PREFACE.  [80]
  [81]
The book called 'The Consolation of Philosophy' was throughout the  [82]
Middle Ages, and down to the beginnings of the modern epoch in the  [83]
sixteenth century, the scholar's familiar companion. Few books have  [84]
exercised a wider influence in their time. It has been translated into  [85]
every European tongue, and into English nearly a dozen times, from King  [86]
Alfred's paraphrase to the translations of Lord Preston, Causton,  [87]
Ridpath, and Duncan, in the eighteenth century. The belief that what  [88]
once pleased so widely must still have some charm is my excuse for  [89]
attempting the present translation. The great work of Boethius, with its  [90]
alternate prose and verse, skilfully fitted together like dialogue and  [91]
chorus in a Greek play, is unique in literature, and has a pathetic  [92]
interest from the time and circumstances of its composition. It ought  [93]
not to be forgotten. Those who can go to the original will find their  [94]
reward. There may be room also for a new translation in English after an  [95]
interval of close on a hundred years.  [96]
  [97]
Some of the editions contain a reproduction of a bust purporting to  [98]
represent Boethius. Lord Preston's translation, for example, has such a  [99]
portrait, which it refers to an original in marble at Rome. This I have  [100]
been unable to trace, and suspect that it is apocryphal. The Hope  [101]
Collection at Oxford contains a completely different portrait in a  [102]
print, which gives no authority. I have ventured to use as a  [103]
frontispiece a reproduction from a plaster-cast in the Ashmolean Museum,  [104]
taken from an ivory diptych preserved in the Bibliotheca Quiriniana at  [105]
Brescia, which represents Narius Manlius Boethius, the father of the  [106]
philosopher. Portraiture of this period is so rare that it seemed that,  [107]
failing a likeness of the author himself, this authentic representation  [108]
of his father might have interest, as giving the consular dress and  [109]
insignia of the time, and also as illustrating the decadence of  [110]
contemporary art. The consul wears a richly-embroidered cloak; his right  [111]
hand holds a staff surmounted by the Roman eagle, his left the _mappa  [112]
circensis,_ or napkin used for starting the races in the circus; at his  [113]
feet are palms and bags of money--prizes for the victors in the games.  [114]
For permission to use this cast my thanks are due to the authorities of  [115]
the Ashmolean Museum, as also to Mr. T.W. Jackson, Curator of the Hope  [116]
Collection, who first called my attention to its existence.  [117]
  [118]
I have to thank my brother, Mr. L. James, of Radley College, for much  [119]
valuable help and for correcting the proof-sheets of the translation.  [120]
The text used is that of Peiper, Leipsic, 1874.  [121]

PROEM.  [126]
  [127]
Anicus Manlius Severinus Boethius lived in the last quarter of the fifth  [128]
century A.D., and the first quarter of the sixth. He was growing to  [129]
manhood, when Theodoric, the famous Ostrogoth, crossed the Alps and made  [130]
himself master of Italy. Boethius belonged to an ancient family, which  [131]
boasted a connection with the legendary glories of the Republic, and was  [132]
still among the foremost in wealth and dignity in the days of Rome's  [133]
abasement. His parents dying early, he was brought up by Symmachus, whom  [134]
the age agreed to regard as of almost saintly character, and afterwards  [135]
became his son-in-law. His varied gifts, aided by an excellent  [136]
education, won for him the reputation of the most accomplished man of  [137]
his time. He was orator, poet, musician, philosopher. It is his peculiar  [138]
distinction to have handed on to the Middle Ages the tradition of Greek  [139]
philosophy by his Latin translations of the works of Aristotle. Called  [140]
early to a public career, the highest honours of the State came to him  [141]
unsought. He was sole Consul in 510 A.D., and was ultimately raised by  [142]
Theodoric to the dignity of Magister Officiorum, or head of the whole  [143]
civil administration. He was no less happy in his domestic life, in the  [144]
virtues of his wife, Rusticiana, and the fair promise of his two sons,  [145]
Symmachus and Boethius; happy also in the society of a refined circle of  [146]
friends. Noble, wealthy, accomplished, universally esteemed for his  [147]
virtues, high in the favour of the Gothic King, he appeared to all men a  [148]
signal example of the union of merit and good fortune. His felicity  [149]
seemed to culminate in the year 522 A.D., when, by special and  [150]
extraordinary favour, his two sons, young as they were for so exalted an  [151]
honour, were created joint Consuls and rode to the senate-house  [152]
attended by a throng of senators, and the acclamations of the multitude.  [153]
Boethius himself, amid the general applause, delivered the public speech  [154]
in the King's honour usual on such occasions. Within a year he was a  [155]
solitary prisoner at Pavia, stripped of honours, wealth, and friends,  [156]
with death hanging over him, and a terror worse than death, in the fear  [157]
lest those dearest to him should be involved in the worst results of his  [158]
downfall. It is in this situation that the opening of the 'Consolation  [159]
of Philosophy' brings Boethius before us. He represents himself as  [160]
seated in his prison distraught with grief, indignant at the injustice  [161]
of his misfortunes, and seeking relief for his melancholy in writing  [162]
verses descriptive of his condition. Suddenly there appears to him the  [163]
Divine figure of Philosophy, in the guise of a woman of superhuman  [164]
dignity and beauty, who by a succession of discourses convinces him of  [165]
the vanity of regret for the lost gifts of fortune, raises his mind once  [166]
more to the contemplation of the true good, and makes clear to him the  [167]
mystery of the world's moral government.  [168]

SUMMARY.  [253]
  [254]
Boethius' complaint (Song I.).--CH. I. Philosophy appears to  [255]
Boethius, drives away the Muses of Poetry, and herself laments  [256]
(Song II.) the disordered condition of his mind.--CH. II. Boethius  [257]
is speechless with amazement. Philosophy wipes away the tears that  [258]
have clouded his eyesight.--CH. III. Boethius recognises his  [259]
mistress Philosophy. To his wondering inquiries she explains her  [260]
presence, and recalls to his mind the persecutions to which  [261]
Philosophy has oftentimes from of old been subjected by an ignorant  [262]
world. CH. IV. Philosophy bids Boethius declare his griefs. He  [263]
relates the story of his unjust accusation and ruin. He concludes  [264]
with a prayer (Song V.) that the moral disorder in human affairs  [265]
may be set right.--CH. V. Philosophy admits the justice of  [266]
Boethius' self-vindication, but grieves rather for the unhappy  [267]
change in his mind. She will first tranquillize his spirit by  [268]
soothing remedies.--CH. VI. Philosophy tests Boethius' mental  [269]
state by certain questions, and discovers three chief causes of his  [270]
soul's sickness: (1) He has forgotten his own true nature; (2) he  [271]
knows not the end towards which the whole universe tends; (3) he  [272]
knows not the means by which the world is governed.  [273]

Smoothly once in happier days,  [288]
Now perforce in tears and sadness  [289]
Learn a mournful strain to raise.  [290]
Lo, the Muses, grief-dishevelled,  [291]
Guide my pen and voice my woe;  [292]
Down their cheeks unfeigned the tear drops  [293]
To my sad complainings flow!  [294]
These alone in danger's hour  [295]
Faithful found, have dared attend  [296]
On the footsteps of the exile  [297]
To his lonely journey's end.  [298]
These that were the pride and pleasure  [299]
Of my youth and high estate  [300]
Still remain the only solace  [301]
Of the old man's mournful fate.  [302]
Old? Ah yes; swift, ere I knew it,  [303]
By these sorrows on me pressed  [304]
Age hath come; lo, Grief hath bid me  [305]
Wear the garb that fits her best.  [306]
O'er my head untimely sprinkled  [307]
These white hairs my woes proclaim,  [308]
And the skin hangs loose and shrivelled  [309]
On this sorrow-shrunken frame.  [310]
Blest is death that intervenes not  [311]
In the sweet, sweet years of peace,  [312]
But unto the broken-hearted,  [313]
When they call him, brings release!  [314]
Yet Death passes by the wretched,  [315]
Shuts his ear and slumbers deep;  [316]
Will not heed the cry of anguish,  [317]
Will not close the eyes that weep.  [318]
For, while yet inconstant Fortune  [319]
Poured her gifts and all was bright,  [320]
Death's dark hour had all but whelmed me  [321]
In the gloom of endless night.  [322]
Now, because misfortune's shadow  [323]
Hath o'erclouded that false face,  [324]
Cruel Life still halts and lingers,  [325]
Though I loathe his weary race.  [326]
Friends, why did ye once so lightly  [327]
Vaunt me happy among men?  [328]
Surely he who so hath fallen  [329]
Was not firmly founded then.  [330]

I.  [334]
  [335]
  [336]
While I was thus mutely pondering within myself, and recording my  [337]
sorrowful complainings with my pen, it seemed to me that there appeared  [338]
above my head a woman of a countenance exceeding venerable. Her eyes  [339]
were bright as fire, and of a more than human keenness; her complexion  [340]
was lively, her vigour showed no trace of enfeeblement; and yet her  [341]
years were right full, and she plainly seemed not of our age and time.  [342]
Her stature was difficult to judge. At one moment it exceeded not the  [343]
common height, at another her forehead seemed to strike the sky; and  [344]
whenever she raised her head higher, she began to pierce within the very  [345]
heavens, and to baffle the eyes of them that looked upon her. Her  [346]
garments were of an imperishable fabric, wrought with the finest threads  [347]
and of the most delicate workmanship; and these, as her own lips  [348]
afterwards assured me, she had herself woven with her own hands. The  [349]
beauty of this vesture had been somewhat tarnished by age and neglect,  [350]
and wore that dingy look which marble contracts from exposure. On the  [351]
lower-most edge was inwoven the Greek letter [Greek: P], on the topmost  [352]
the letter [Greek: Th],[A] and between the two were to be seen steps,  [353]
like a staircase, from the lower to the upper letter. This robe,  [354]
moreover, had been torn by the hands of violent persons, who had each  [355]
snatched away what he could clutch.[B] Her right hand held a note-book;  [356]
in her left she bore a staff. And when she saw the Muses of Poesie  [357]
standing by my bedside, dictating the words of my lamentations, she was  [358]
moved awhile to wrath, and her eyes flashed sternly. 'Who,' said she,  [359]
'has allowed yon play-acting wantons to approach this sick man--these  [360]
who, so far from giving medicine to heal his malady, even feed it with  [361]
sweet poison? These it is who kill the rich crop of reason with the  [362]
barren thorns of passion, who accustom men's minds to disease, instead  [363]
of setting them free. Now, were it some common man whom your allurements  [364]
were seducing, as is usually your way, I should be less indignant. On  [365]
such a one I should not have spent my pains for naught. But this is one  [366]
nurtured in the Eleatic and Academic philosophies. Nay, get ye gone, ye  [367]
sirens, whose sweetness lasteth not; leave him for my muses to tend and  [368]
heal!' At these words of upbraiding, the whole band, in deepened  [369]
sadness, with downcast eyes, and blushes that confessed their shame,  [370]
dolefully left the chamber.  [371]
  [372]
But I, because my sight was dimmed with much weeping, and I could not  [373]
tell who was this woman of authority so commanding--I was dumfoundered,  [374]
and, with my gaze fastened on the earth, continued silently to await  [375]
what she might do next. Then she drew near me and sat on the edge of my  [376]
couch, and, looking into my face all heavy with grief and fixed in  [377]
sadness on the ground, she bewailed in these words the disorder of my  [378]
mind:  [379]

  [394]
  [395]
Alas! in what abyss his mind  [396]
Is plunged, how wildly tossed!  [397]
Still, still towards the outer night  [398]
She sinks, her true light lost,  [399]
As oft as, lashed tumultuously  [400]
By earth-born blasts, care's waves rise high.  [401]
  [402]
Yet once he ranged the open heavens,  [403]
The sun's bright pathway tracked;  [404]
Watched how the cold moon waxed and waned;  [405]
Nor rested, till there lacked  [406]
To his wide ken no star that steers  [407]
Amid the maze of circling spheres.  [408]
  [409]
The causes why the blusterous winds  [410]
Vex ocean's tranquil face,  [411]
Whose hand doth turn the stable globe,  [412]
Or why his even race  [413]
From out the ruddy east the sun  [414]
Unto the western waves doth run:  [415]
  [416]
What is it tempers cunningly  [417]
The placid hours of spring,  [418]
So that it blossoms with the rose  [419]
For earth's engarlanding:  [420]
Who loads the year's maturer prime  [421]
With clustered grapes in autumn time:  [422]

Deep Nature's lore to guess.  [425]
Now, reft of reason's light, he lies,  [426]
And bonds his neck oppress;  [427]
While by the heavy load constrained,  [428]
His eyes to this dull earth are chained.  [429]

II.  [433]
  [434]
  [435]
'But the time,' said she, 'calls rather for healing than for  [436]
lamentation.' Then, with her eyes bent full upon me, 'Art thou that  [437]
man,' she cries, 'who, erstwhile fed with the milk and reared upon the  [438]
nourishment which is mine to give, had grown up to the full vigour of a  [439]
manly spirit? And yet I had bestowed such armour on thee as would have  [440]
proved an invincible defence, hadst thou not first cast it away. Dost  [441]
thou know me? Why art thou silent? Is it shame or amazement that hath  [442]
struck thee dumb? Would it were shame; but, as I see, a stupor hath  [443]
seized upon thee.' Then, when she saw me not only answering nothing, but  [444]
mute and utterly incapable of speech, she gently touched my breast with  [445]
her hand, and said: 'There is no danger; these are the symptoms of  [446]
lethargy, the usual sickness of deluded minds. For awhile he has  [447]
forgotten himself; he will easily recover his memory, if only he first  [448]
recognises me. And that he may do so, let me now wipe his eyes that are  [449]
clouded with a mist of mortal things.' Thereat, with a fold of her robe,  [450]
she dried my eyes all swimming with tears.  [451]

III.  [473]
  [474]
  [475]
Even so the clouds of my melancholy were broken up. I saw the clear sky,  [476]
and regained the power to recognise the face of my physician.  [477]
Accordingly, when I had lifted my eyes and fixed my gaze upon her, I  [478]
beheld my nurse, Philosophy, whose halls I had frequented from my youth  [479]
up.  [480]
  [481]
'Ah! why,' I cried, 'mistress of all excellence, hast thou come down  [482]
from on high, and entered the solitude of this my exile? Is it that  [483]
thou, too, even as I, mayst be persecuted with false accusations?'  [484]
  [485]
'Could I desert thee, child,' said she, 'and not lighten the burden  [486]
which thou hast taken upon thee through the hatred of my name, by  [487]
sharing this trouble? Even forgetting that it were not lawful for  [488]
Philosophy to leave companionless the way of the innocent, should I,  [489]
thinkest thou, fear to incur reproach, or shrink from it, as though  [490]
some strange new thing had befallen? Thinkest thou that now, for the  [491]
first time in an evil age, Wisdom hath been assailed by peril? Did I not  [492]
often in days of old, before my servant Plato lived, wage stern warfare  [493]
with the rashness of folly? In his lifetime, too, Socrates, his master,  [494]
won with my aid the victory of an unjust death. And when, one after the  [495]
other, the Epicurean herd, the Stoic, and the rest, each of them as far  [496]
as in them lay, went about to seize the heritage he left, and were  [497]
dragging me off protesting and resisting, as their booty, they tore in  [498]

Summary  [1007]
  [1008]
CH. I. Philosophy reproves Boethius for the foolishness of his  [1009]
complaints against Fortune. Her very nature is caprice.--CH. II.  [1010]
Philosophy in Fortune's name replies to Boethius' reproaches, and  [1011]
proves that the gifts of Fortune are hers to give and to take  [1012]
away.--CH. III. Boethius falls back upon his present sense of  [1013]
misery. Philosophy reminds him of the brilliancy of his former  [1014]
fortunes.--CH. IV. Boethius objects that the memory of past  [1015]
happiness is the bitterest portion of the lot of the unhappy.  [1016]
Philosophy shows that much is still left for which he may be  [1017]
thankful. None enjoy perfect satisfaction with their lot. But  [1018]
happiness depends not on anything which Fortune can give. It is to  [1019]
be sought within.--CH. V. All the gifts of Fortune are external;  [1020]
they can never truly be our own. Man cannot find his good in  [1021]
worldly possessions. Riches bring anxiety and trouble.--CH. VI.  [1022]
High place without virtue is an evil, not a good. Power is an empty  [1023]
name.--CH. VII. Fame is a thing of little account when compared  [1024]
with the immensity of the Universe and the endlessness of  [1025]
Time.--CH. VIII. One service only can Fortune do, when she reveals  [1026]
her own nature and distinguishes true friends from false.  [1027]

SUMMARY  [1856]
  [1857]
CH. I. Boethius beseeches Philosophy to continue. She promises to  [1858]
lead him to true happiness.--CH. II. Happiness is the one end which  [1859]
all created beings seek. They aim variously at (_a_) wealth, or  [1860]
(_b_) rank, or (_c_) sovereignty, or (_d_) glory, or (_e_)  [1861]
pleasure, because they think thereby to attain either (_a_)  [1862]
contentment, (_b_) reverence, (_c_) power, (_d_) renown, or (_e_)  [1863]
gladness of heart, in one or other of which they severally imagine  [1864]
happiness to consist.--CH. III. Philosophy proceeds to consider  [1865]
whether happiness can really be secured in any of these ways, (_a_)  [1866]
So far from bringing contentment, riches only add to men's  [1867]
wants.--CH. IV. (_b_) High position cannot of itself win respect.  [1868]
Titles command no reverence in distant and barbarous lands. They  [1869]
even fall into contempt through lapse of time.--CH. V. (_c_)  [1870]
Sovereignty cannot even bestow safety. History tells of the  [1871]
downfall of kings and their ministers. Tyrants go in fear of their  [1872]
lives. --CH. VI. (_d_) Fame conferred on the unworthy is but  [1873]
disgrace. The splendour of noble birth is not a man's own, but his  [1874]
ancestors'.--CH. VII. (_e_) Pleasure begins in the restlessness of  [1875]
desire, and ends in repentance. Even the pure pleasures of home may  [1876]
turn to gall and bitterness.--CH. VIII. All fail, then, to give  [1877]
what they promise. There is, moreover, some accompanying evil  [1878]
involved in each of these aims. Beauty and bodily strength are  [1879]
likewise of little worth. In strength man is surpassed by the  [1880]
brutes; beauty is but outward show.--CH. IX. The source of men's  [1881]
error in following these phantoms of good is that _they break up  [1882]
and separate that which is in its nature one and indivisible_.  [1883]
Contentment, power, reverence, renown, and joy are essentially  [1884]
bound up one with the other, and, if they are to be attained at  [1885]
all, must be attained _together_. True happiness, if it can be  [1886]
found, will include them all. But it cannot be found among the  [1887]
perishable things hitherto considered.--CH. X. Such a happiness  [1888]
necessarily exists. Its seat is in God. Nay, God is very happiness,  [1889]
and in a manner, therefore, the happy man partakes also of the  [1890]
Divine nature. All other ends are relative to this good, since they  [1891]
are all pursued only for the sake of good; it is _good_ which is  [1892]
the sole ultimate end. And since the sole end is also happiness, it  [1893]
is plain that this good and happiness are in essence the same.--CH.  [1894]
XI. Unity is another aspect of goodness. Now, all things subsist so  [1895]
long only as they preserve the unity of their being; when they lose  [1896]
this unity, they perish. But the bent of nature forces all things  [1897]
(plants and inanimate things, as well as animals) to strive to  [1898]
continue in life. Therefore, all things desire unity, for unity is  [1899]
essential to life. But unity and goodness were shown to be the  [1900]
same. Therefore, good is proved to be the end towards which the  [1901]
whole universe tends.[E]--CH. XII. Boethius acknowledges that he is  [1902]
but recollecting truths he once knew. Philosophy goes on to show  [1903]
that it is goodness also by which the whole world is governed.[F]  [1904]
Boethius professes compunction for his former folly. But the  [1905]
paradox of evil is introduced, and he is once more perplexed.  [1906]

SONG IX.[I]  [2698]
  [2699]
INVOCATION.  [2700]
  [2701]
  [2702]
Maker of earth and sky, from age to age  [2703]
Who rul'st the world by reason; at whose word  [2704]
Time issues from Eternity's abyss:  [2705]
To all that moves the source of movement, fixed  [2706]
Thyself and moveless. Thee no cause impelled  [2707]
Extrinsic this proportioned frame to shape  [2708]
From shapeless matter; but, deep-set within  [2709]
Thy inmost being, the form of perfect good,  [2710]
From envy free; and Thou didst mould the whole  [2711]
To that supernal pattern. Beauteous  [2712]
The world in Thee thus imaged, being Thyself  [2713]
  [2714]
  [2715]
Most beautiful. So Thou the work didst fashion  [2716]
In that fair likeness, bidding it put on  [2717]
Perfection through the exquisite perfectness  [2718]
Of every part's contrivance. Thou dost bind  [2719]
The elements in balanced harmony,  [2720]
So that the hot and cold, the moist and dry,  [2721]
Contend not; nor the pure fire leaping up  [2722]
Escape, or weight of waters whelm the earth.  [2723]
  [2724]
Thou joinest and diffusest through the whole,  [2725]
Linking accordantly its several parts,  [2726]
A soul of threefold nature, moving all.  [2727]
This, cleft in twain, and in two circles gathered,  [2728]
Speeds in a path that on itself returns,  [2729]
Encompassing mind's limits, and conforms  [2730]
The heavens to her true semblance. Lesser souls  [2731]
And lesser lives by a like ordinance  [2732]
Thou sendest forth, each to its starry car  [2733]
Affixing, and dost strew them far and wide  [2734]
O'er earth and heaven. These by a law benign  [2735]
Thou biddest turn again, and render back  [2736]
To thee their fires. Oh, grant, almighty Father,  [2737]
Grant us on reason's wing to soar aloft  [2738]
To heaven's exalted height; grant us to see  [2739]
The fount of good; grant us, the true light found,  [2740]
To fix our steadfast eyes in vision clear  [2741]
On Thee. Disperse the heavy mists of earth,  [2742]
And shine in Thine own splendour. For Thou art  [2743]
The true serenity and perfect rest  [2744]
Of every pious soul--to see Thy face,  [2745]
The end and the beginning--One the guide,  [2746]
The traveller, the pathway, and the goal.  [2747]

X.  [2756]
  [2757]
  [2758]
'Since now thou hast seen what is the form of the imperfect good, and  [2759]
what the form of the perfect also, methinks I should next show in what  [2760]
manner this perfection of felicity is built up. And here I conceive it  [2761]
proper to inquire, first, whether any excellence, such as thou hast  [2762]
lately defined, can exist in the nature of things, lest we be deceived  [2763]
by an empty fiction of thought to which no true reality answers. But it  [2764]
cannot be denied that such does exist, and is, as it were, the source of  [2765]
all things good. For everything which is called imperfect is spoken of  [2766]
as imperfect by reason of the privation of some perfection; so it comes  [2767]
to pass that, whenever imperfection is found in any particular, there  [2768]
must necessarily be a perfection in respect of that particular also. For  [2769]
were there no such perfection, it is utterly inconceivable how that  [2770]
so-called _im_perfection should come into existence. Nature does not  [2771]
make a beginning with things mutilated and imperfect; she starts with  [2772]
what is whole and perfect, and falls away later to these feeble and  [2773]
inferior productions. So if there is, as we showed before, a happiness  [2774]
of a frail and imperfect kind, it cannot be doubted but there is also a  [2775]
happiness substantial and perfect.'  [2776]
  [2777]
'Most true is thy conclusion, and most sure,' said I.  [2778]
  [2779]
'Next to consider where the dwelling-place of this happiness may be. The  [2780]
common belief of all mankind agrees that God, the supreme of all things,  [2781]
is good. For since nothing can be imagined better than God, how can we  [2782]
doubt Him to be good than whom there is nothing better? Now, reason  [2783]
shows God to be good in such wise as to prove that in Him is perfect  [2784]
good. For were it not so, He would not be supreme of all things; for  [2785]
there would be something else more excellent, possessed of perfect good,  [2786]
which would seem to have the advantage in priority and dignity, since it  [2787]
has clearly appeared that all perfect things are prior to those less  [2788]
complete. Wherefore, lest we fall into an infinite regression, we must  [2789]
acknowledge the supreme God to be full of supreme and perfect good. But  [2790]
we have determined that true happiness is the perfect good; therefore  [2791]
true happiness must dwell in the supreme Deity.'  [2792]
  [2793]
'I accept thy reasonings,' said I; 'they cannot in any wise be  [2794]
disputed.'  [2795]
  [2796]
'But, come, see how strictly and incontrovertibly thou mayst prove this  [2797]
our assertion that the supreme Godhead hath fullest possession of the  [2798]
highest good.'  [2799]
  [2800]
'In what way, pray?' said I.  [2801]
  [2802]
'Do not rashly suppose that He who is the Father of all things hath  [2803]
received that highest good of which He is said to be possessed either  [2804]
from some external source, or hath it as a natural endowment in such  [2805]
sort that thou mightest consider the essence of the happiness possessed,  [2806]
and of the God who possesses it, distinct and different. For if thou  [2807]
deemest it received from without, thou mayst esteem that which gives  [2808]
more excellent than that which has received. But Him we most worthily  [2809]
acknowledge to be the most supremely excellent of all things. If,  [2810]
however, it is in Him by nature, yet is logically distinct, the thought  [2811]
is inconceivable, since we are speaking of God, who is supreme of all  [2812]
things. Who was there to join these distinct essences? Finally, when one  [2813]
thing is different from another, the things so conceived as distinct  [2814]
cannot be identical. Therefore that which of its own nature is distinct  [2815]
from the highest good is not itself the highest good--an impious thought  [2816]
of Him than whom, 'tis plain, nothing can be more excellent. For  [2817]
universally nothing can be better in nature than the source from which  [2818]
it has come; therefore on most true grounds of reason would I conclude  [2819]
that which is the source of all things to be in its own essence the  [2820]
highest good.'  [2821]
  [2822]
'And most justly,' said I.  [2823]
  [2824]
'But the highest good has been admitted to be happiness.'  [2825]
  [2826]
'Yes.'  [2827]
  [2828]
'Then,' said she, 'it is necessary to acknowledge that God is very  [2829]
happiness.'  [2830]
  [2831]
'Yes,' said I; 'I cannot gainsay my former admissions, and I see clearly  [2832]
that this is a necessary inference therefrom.'  [2833]
  [2834]
'Reflect, also,' said she, 'whether the same conclusion is not further  [2835]
confirmed by considering that there cannot be two supreme goods distinct  [2836]
one from the other. For the goods which are different clearly cannot be  [2837]
severally each what the other is: wherefore neither of the two can be  [2838]
perfect, since to either the other is wanting; but since it is not  [2839]
perfect, it cannot manifestly be the supreme good. By no means, then,  [2840]
can goods which are supreme be different one from the other. But we have  [2841]
concluded that both happiness and God are the supreme good; wherefore  [2842]
that which is highest Divinity must also itself necessarily be supreme  [2843]
happiness.'  [2844]
  [2845]
'No conclusion,' said I, 'could be truer to fact, nor more soundly  [2846]
reasoned out, nor more worthy of God.'  [2847]
  [2848]
'Then, further,' said she, 'just as geometricians are wont to draw  [2849]
inferences from their demonstrations to which they give the name  [2850]
"deductions," so will I add here a sort of corollary. For since men  [2851]
become happy by the acquisition of happiness, while happiness is very  [2852]
Godship, it is manifest that they become happy by the acquisition of  [2853]
Godship. But as by the acquisition of justice men become just, and wise  [2854]
by the acquisition of wisdom, so by parity of reasoning by acquiring  [2855]
Godship they must of necessity become gods. So every man who is happy is  [2856]
a god; and though in nature God is One only, yet there is nothing to  [2857]
hinder that very many should be gods by participation in that nature.'  [2858]
  [2859]
'A fair conclusion, and a precious,' said I, 'deduction or corollary, by  [2860]
whichever name thou wilt call it.'  [2861]
  [2862]
'And yet,' said she, 'not one whit fairer than this which reason  [2863]
persuades us to add.'  [2864]
  [2865]
'Why, what?' said I.  [2866]
  [2867]
'Why, seeing happiness has many particulars included under it, should  [2868]
all these be regarded as forming one body of happiness, as it were, made  [2869]
up of various parts, or is there some one of them which forms the full  [2870]
essence of happiness, while all the rest are relative to this?'  [2871]
  [2872]
'I would thou wouldst unfold the whole matter to me at large.'  [2873]
  [2874]
'We judge happiness to be good, do we not?'  [2875]
  [2876]
'Yea, the supreme good.'  [2877]
  [2878]
'And this superlative applies to all; for this same happiness is  [2879]
adjudged to be the completest independence, the highest power,  [2880]
reverence, renown, and pleasure.'  [2881]
  [2882]
'What then?'  [2883]
  [2884]
'Are all these goods--independence, power, and the rest--to be deemed  [2885]
members of happiness, as it were, or are they all relative to good as to  [2886]
their summit and crown?'  [2887]
  [2888]
'I understand the problem, but I desire to hear how thou wouldst solve  [2889]
it.'  [2890]
  [2891]
'Well, then, listen to the determination of the matter. Were all these  [2892]
members composing happiness, they would differ severally one from the  [2893]
other. For this is the nature of parts--that by their difference they  [2894]
compose one body. All these, however, have been proved to be the same;  [2895]
therefore they cannot possibly be members, otherwise happiness will seem  [2896]
to be built up out of one member, which cannot be.'  [2897]
  [2898]
'There can be no doubt as to that,' said I; 'but I am impatient to hear  [2899]
what remains.'  [2900]
  [2901]
'Why, it is manifest that all the others are relative to the good. For  [2902]
the very reason why independence is sought is that it is judged good,  [2903]
and so power also, because it is believed to be good. The same, too, may  [2904]
be supposed of reverence, of renown, and of pleasant delight. Good,  [2905]
then, is the sum and source of all desirable things. That which has not  [2906]
in itself any good, either in reality or in semblance, can in no wise be  [2907]
desired. Contrariwise, even things which by nature are not good are  [2908]
desired as if they were truly good, if they seem to be so. Whereby it  [2909]
comes to pass that goodness is rightly believed to be the sum and hinge  [2910]
and cause of all things desirable. Now, that for the sake of which  [2911]
anything is desired itself seems to be most wished for. For instance, if  [2912]
anyone wishes to ride for the sake of health, he does not so much wish  [2913]
for the exercise of riding as the benefit of his health. Since, then,  [2914]
all things are sought for the sake of the good, it is not these so much  [2915]
as good itself that is sought by all. But that on account of which all  [2916]
other things are wished for was, we agreed, happiness; wherefore thus  [2917]
also it appears that it is happiness alone which is sought. From all  [2918]
which it is transparently clear that the essence of absolute good and of  [2919]
happiness is one and the same.'  [2920]
  [2921]
'I cannot see how anyone can dissent from these conclusions.'  [2922]
  [2923]
'But we have also proved that God and true happiness are one and the  [2924]
same.'  [2925]
  [2926]
'Yes,' said I.  [2927]
  [2928]
'Then we can safely conclude, also, that God's essence is seated in  [2929]
absolute good, and nowhere else.'  [2930]

SONG X.  [2934]
  [2935]
THE TRUE LIGHT.  [2936]
  [2937]
  [2938]
Hither come, all ye whose minds  [2939]
Lust with rosy fetters binds--  [2940]
Lust to bondage hard compelling  [2941]
Th' earthy souls that are his dwelling--  [2942]
Here shall be your labour's close;  [2943]
Here your haven of repose.  [2944]
Come, to your one refuge press;  [2945]
Wide it stands to all distress!  [2946]
  [2947]
Not the glint of yellow gold  [2948]
Down bright Hermus' current rolled;  [2949]
Not the Tagus' precious sands,  [2950]
Nor in far-off scorching lands  [2951]
All the radiant gems that hide  [2952]
Under Indus' storied tide--  [2953]
Emerald green and glistering white--  [2954]
Can illume our feeble sight;  [2955]
But they rather leave the mind  [2956]
In its native darkness blind.  [2957]
For the fairest beams they shed  [2958]
In earth's lowest depths were fed;  [2959]
But the splendour that supplies  [2960]
Strength and vigour to the skies,  [2961]
And the universe controls,  [2962]
Shunneth dark and ruined souls.  [2963]
He who once hath seen _this_ light  [2964]
Will not call the sunbeam bright.  [2965]

XI.  [2969]
  [2970]
  [2971]
'I quite agree,' said I, 'truly all thy reasonings hold admirably  [2972]
together.'  [2973]
  [2974]
Then said she: 'What value wouldst thou put upon the boon shouldst thou  [2975]
come to the knowledge of the absolute good?'  [2976]
  [2977]
'Oh, an infinite,' said I, 'if only I were so blest as to learn to know  [2978]
God also who is the good.'  [2979]
  [2980]
'Yet this will I make clear to thee on truest grounds of reason, if only  [2981]
our recent conclusions stand fast.'  [2982]
  [2983]
'They will.'  [2984]
  [2985]
'Have we not shown that those things which most men desire are not true  [2986]
and perfect good precisely for this cause--that they differ severally  [2987]
one from another, and, seeing that one is wanting to another, they  [2988]
cannot bestow full and absolute good; but that they become the true good  [2989]
when they are gathered, as it were, into one form and agency, so that  [2990]
that which is independence is likewise power, reverence, renown, and  [2991]
pleasant delight, and unless they are all one and the same, they have no  [2992]
claim to be counted among things desirable?'  [2993]
  [2994]
'Yes; this was clearly proved, and cannot in any wise be doubted.'  [2995]
  [2996]
'Now, when things are far from being good while they are different, but  [2997]
become good as soon as they are one, is it not true that these become  [2998]
good by acquiring unity?'  [2999]
  [3000]
'It seems so,' said I.  [3001]
  [3002]
'But dost not thou allow that all which is good is good by participation  [3003]
in goodness?'  [3004]
  [3005]
'It is.'  [3006]
  [3007]
'Then, thou must on similar grounds admit that unity and goodness are  [3008]
the same; for when the effects of things in their natural working differ  [3009]
not, their essence is one and the same.'  [3010]
  [3011]
'There is no denying it.'  [3012]
  [3013]
'Now, dost thou know,' said she, 'that all which is abides and subsists  [3014]
so long as it continues one, but so soon as it ceases to be one it  [3015]
perishes and falls to pieces?'  [3016]
  [3017]
'In what way?'  [3018]
  [3019]
'Why, take animals, for example. When soul and body come together, and  [3020]
continue in one, this is, we say, a living creature; but when this unity  [3021]
is broken by the separation of these two, the creature dies, and is  [3022]
clearly no longer living. The body also, while it remains in one form by  [3023]
the joining together of its members, presents a human appearance; but if  [3024]
the separation and dispersal of the parts break up the body's unity, it  [3025]
ceases to be what it was. And if we extend our survey to all other  [3026]
things, without doubt it will manifestly appear that each several thing  [3027]
subsists while it is one, but when it ceases to be one perishes.'  [3028]
  [3029]
'Yes; when I consider further, I see it to be even as thou sayest.'  [3030]
  [3031]
'Well, is there aught,' said she, 'which, in so far as it acts  [3032]
conformably to nature, abandons the wish for life, and desires to come  [3033]
to death and corruption?'  [3034]
  [3035]
'Looking to living creatures, which have some faults of choice, I find  [3036]
none that, without external compulsion, forego the will to live, and of  [3037]
their own accord hasten to destruction. For every creature diligently  [3038]
pursues the end of self-preservation, and shuns death and destruction!  [3039]
As to herbs and trees, and inanimate things generally, I am altogether  [3040]
in doubt what to think.'  [3041]
  [3042]
'And yet there is no possibility of question about this either, since  [3043]
thou seest how herbs and trees grow in places suitable for them, where,  [3044]
as far as their nature admits, they cannot quickly wither and die. Some  [3045]
spring up in the plains, others in the mountains; some grow in marshes,  [3046]
others cling to rocks; and others, again, find a fertile soil in the  [3047]
barren sands; and if you try to transplant these elsewhere, they wither  [3048]
away. Nature gives to each the soil that suits it, and uses her  [3049]
diligence to prevent any of them dying, so long as it is possible for  [3050]
them to continue alive. Why do they all draw their nourishment from  [3051]
roots as from a mouth dipped into the earth, and distribute the strong  [3052]
bark over the pith? Why are all the softer parts like the pith deeply  [3053]
encased within, while the external parts have the strong texture of  [3054]
wood, and outside of all is the bark to resist the weather's  [3055]
inclemency, like a champion stout in endurance? Again, how great is  [3056]
nature's diligence to secure universal propagation by multiplying seed!  [3057]
Who does not know all these to be contrivances, not only for the present  [3058]
maintenance of a species, but for its lasting continuance, generation  [3059]
after generation, for ever? And do not also the things believed  [3060]
inanimate on like grounds of reason seek each what is proper to itself?  [3061]
Why do the flames shoot lightly upward, while the earth presses downward  [3062]
with its weight, if it is not that these motions and situations are  [3063]
suitable to their respective natures? Moreover, each several thing is  [3064]
preserved by that which is agreeable to its nature, even as it is  [3065]
destroyed by things inimical. Things solid like stones resist  [3066]
disintegration by the close adhesion of their parts. Things fluid like  [3067]
air and water yield easily to what divides them, but swiftly flow back  [3068]
and mingle with those parts from which they have been severed, while  [3069]
fire, again, refuses to be cut at all. And we are not now treating of  [3070]
the voluntary motions of an intelligent soul, but of the drift of  [3071]
nature. Even so is it that we digest our food without thinking about it,  [3072]
and draw our breath unconsciously in sleep; nay, even in living  [3073]
creatures the love of life cometh not of conscious will, but from the  [3074]
principles of nature. For oftentimes in the stress of circumstances will  [3075]
chooses the death which nature shrinks from; and contrarily, in spite of  [3076]
natural appetite, will restrains that work of reproduction by which  [3077]
alone the persistence of perishable creatures is maintained. So entirely  [3078]
does this love of self come from drift of nature, not from animal  [3079]
impulse. Providence has furnished things with this most cogent reason  [3080]
for continuance: they must desire life, so long as it is naturally  [3081]
possible for them to continue living. Wherefore in no way mayst thou  [3082]
doubt but that things naturally aim at continuance of existence, and  [3083]
shun destruction.'  [3084]
  [3085]
'I confess,' said I, 'that what I lately thought uncertain, I now  [3086]
perceive to be indubitably clear.'  [3087]
  [3088]
'Now, that which seeks to subsist and continue desires to be one; for if  [3089]
its oneness be gone, its very existence cannot continue.'  [3090]
  [3091]
'True,' said I.  [3092]
  [3093]
'All things, then, desire to be one.'  [3094]
  [3095]
'I agree.'  [3096]
  [3097]
'But we have proved that one is the very same thing as good.'  [3098]
  [3099]
'We have.'  [3100]
  [3101]
'All things, then, seek the good; indeed, you may express the fact by  [3102]
defining good as that which all desire.'  [3103]
  [3104]
'Nothing could be more truly thought out. Either there is no single end  [3105]
to which all things are relative, or else the end to which all things  [3106]
universally hasten must be the highest good of all.'  [3107]
  [3108]
Then she: 'Exceedingly do I rejoice, dear pupil; thine eye is now fixed  [3109]
on the very central mark of truth. Moreover, herein is revealed that of  [3110]
which thou didst erstwhile profess thyself ignorant.'  [3111]
  [3112]
'What is that?' said I.  [3113]
  [3114]
'The end and aim of the whole universe. Surely it is that which is  [3115]
desired of all; and, since we have concluded the good to be such, we  [3116]
ought to acknowledge the end and aim of the whole universe to be "the  [3117]
good."'  [3118]

Who truth pursues, who from false ways  [3127]
His heedful steps would keep,  [3128]
By inward light must search within  [3129]
In meditation deep;  [3130]
All outward bent he must repress  [3131]

  [3133]

  [3140]

XII.  [3157]
  [3158]
  [3159]

reach that goal to which the aim of nature leads and almost constrains  [3647]
them. What if they were left without this mighty, this well-nigh  [3648]
irresistible help of nature's guidance! Consider also how momentous is  [3649]
the powerlessness which incapacitates the wicked. Not light or  [3650]
trivial[L] are the prizes which they contend for, but which they cannot  [3651]
win or hold; nay, their failure concerns the very sum and crown of  [3652]
things. Poor wretches! they fail to compass even that for which they  [3653]
toil day and night. Herein also the strength of the good conspicuously  [3654]
appears. For just as thou wouldst judge him to be the strongest walker  [3655]
whose legs could carry him to a point beyond which no further advance  [3656]
was possible, so must thou needs account him strong in power who so  [3657]
attains the end of his desires that nothing further to be desired lies  [3658]
beyond. Whence follows the obvious conclusion that they who are wicked  [3659]
are seen likewise to be wholly destitute of strength. For why do they  [3660]
forsake virtue and follow vice? Is it from ignorance of what is good?  [3661]
Well, what is more weak and feeble than the blindness of ignorance? Do  [3662]
they know what they ought to follow, but lust drives them aside out of  [3663]
the way? If it be so, they are still frail by reason of their  [3664]
incontinence, for they cannot fight against vice. Or do they knowingly  [3665]
and wilfully forsake the good and turn aside to vice? Why, at this rate,  [3666]
they not only cease to have power, but cease to be at all. For they who  [3667]
forsake the common end of all things that are, they likewise also cease  [3668]
to be at all. Now, to some it may seem strange that we should assert  [3669]
that the bad, who form the greater part of mankind, do not exist. But  [3670]
the fact is so. I do not, indeed, deny that they who are bad are bad,  [3671]
but that they _are_ in an unqualified and absolute sense I deny. Just as  [3672]
we call a corpse a dead man, but cannot call it simply "man," so I would  [3673]
allow the vicious to be bad, but that they _are_ in an absolute sense I  [3674]
cannot allow. That only _is_ which maintains its place and keeps its  [3675]
nature; whatever falls away from this forsakes the existence which is  [3676]
essential to its nature. "But," thou wilt say, "the bad have an  [3677]
ability." Nor do I wish to deny it; only this ability of theirs comes  [3678]
not from strength, but from impotence. For their ability is to do evil,  [3679]
which would have had no efficacy at all if they could have continued in  [3680]
the performance of good. So this ability of theirs proves them still  [3681]
more plainly to have no power. For if, as we concluded just now, evil is  [3682]
nothing, 'tis clear that the wicked can effect nothing, since they are  [3683]
only able to do evil.'  [3684]
  [3685]
''Tis evident.'  [3686]

'We did,' said I.  [3692]
  [3693]
'But that same highest good cannot do evil?'  [3694]

'Certainly not.'  [3696]
  [3697]
'Is there anyone, then, who thinks that men are able to do all things?'  [3698]

'None but a madman.'  [3700]
  [3701]

  [3703]
'Ay; would they could not!'  [3704]
  [3705]
'Since, then, he who can do only good is omnipotent, while they who can  [3706]

evil have less power. There is this also: we have shown that all power  [3708]
is to be reckoned among things desirable, and that all desirable things  [3709]
are referred to good as to a kind of consummation of their nature. But  [3710]
the ability to commit crime cannot be referred to the good; therefore it  [3711]

III.  [3757]
  [3758]
  [3759]
'Thou seest, then, in what foulness unrighteous deeds are sunk, with  [3760]
what splendour righteousness shines. Whereby it is manifest that  [3761]
goodness never lacks its reward, nor crime its punishment. For, verily,  [3762]
in all manner of transactions that for the sake of which the particular  [3763]
action is done may justly be accounted the reward of that action, even  [3764]
as the wreath for the sake of which the race is run is the reward  [3765]
offered for running. Now, we have shown happiness to be that very good  [3766]
for the sake of which all things are done. Absolute good, then, is  [3767]
offered as the common prize, as it were, of all human actions. But,  [3768]
truly, this is a reward from which it is impossible to separate the good  [3769]
man, for one who is without good cannot properly be called good at all;  [3770]
wherefore righteous dealing never misses its reward. Rage the wicked,  [3771]
then, never so violently, the crown shall not fall from the head of the  [3772]
wise, nor wither. Verily, other men's unrighteousness cannot pluck from  [3773]
righteous souls their proper glory. Were the reward in which the soul of  [3774]
the righteous delighteth received from without, then might it be taken  [3775]
away by him who gave it, or some other; but since it is conferred by his  [3776]
own righteousness, then only will he lose his prize when he has ceased  [3777]
to be righteous. Lastly, since every prize is desired because it is  [3778]
believed to be good, who can account him who possesses good to be  [3779]
without reward? And what a prize, the fairest and grandest of all! For  [3780]
remember the corollary which I chiefly insisted on a little while back,  [3781]
and reason thus: Since absolute good is happiness, 'tis clear that all  [3782]
the good must be happy for the very reason that they are good. But it  [3783]
was agreed that those who are happy are gods. So, then, the prize of the  [3784]
good is one which no time may impair, no man's power lessen, no man's  [3785]
unrighteousness tarnish; 'tis very Godship. And this being so, the wise  [3786]
man cannot doubt that punishment is inseparable from the bad. For since  [3787]
good and bad, and likewise reward and punishment, are contraries, it  [3788]
necessarily follows that, corresponding to all that we see accrue as  [3789]
reward of the good, there is some penalty attached as punishment of  [3790]
evil. As, then, righteousness itself is the reward of the righteous, so  [3791]
wickedness itself is the punishment of the unrighteous. Now, no one who  [3792]
is visited with punishment doubts that he is visited with evil.  [3793]
Accordingly, if they were but willing to weigh their own case, could  [3794]
_they_ think themselves free from punishment whom wickedness, worst of  [3795]
all evils, has not only touched, but deeply tainted?  [3796]
  [3797]
'See, also, from the opposite standpoint--the standpoint of the  [3798]
good--what a penalty attends upon the wicked. Thou didst learn a little  [3799]
since that whatever is is one, and that unity itself is good.  [3800]
Accordingly, by this way of reckoning, whatever falls away from goodness  [3801]
ceases to be; whence it comes to pass that the bad cease to be what they  [3802]
were, while only the outward aspect is still left to show they have been  [3803]
men. Wherefore, by their perversion to badness, they have lost their  [3804]
true human nature. Further, since righteousness alone can raise men  [3805]
above the level of humanity, it must needs be that unrighteousness  [3806]
degrades below man's level those whom it has cast out of man's estate.  [3807]

transformed by vice. The violent despoiler of other men's goods,  [3809]
enflamed with covetousness, surely resembles a wolf. A bold and restless  [3810]
spirit, ever wrangling in law-courts, is like some yelping cur. The  [3811]
secret schemer, taking pleasure in fraud and stealth, is own brother to  [3812]
the fox. The passionate man, phrenzied with rage, we might believe to be  [3813]
animated with the soul of a lion. The coward and runaway, afraid where  [3814]
no fear is, may be likened to the timid deer. He who is sunk in  [3815]
ignorance and stupidity lives like a dull ass. He who is light and  [3816]

bird. He who wallows in foul and unclean lusts is sunk in the pleasures  [3818]
of a filthy hog. So it comes to pass that he who by forsaking  [3819]
righteousness ceases to be a man cannot pass into a Godlike condition,  [3820]
but actually turns into a brute beast.'  [3821]

VI.  [4178]
  [4179]
  [4180]
'True,' said I; 'but, since it is thy office to unfold the hidden cause  [4181]
of things, and explain principles veiled in darkness, inform me, I pray  [4182]
thee, of thine own conclusions in this matter, since the marvel of it is  [4183]
what more than aught else disturbs my mind.'  [4184]
  [4185]
A smile played one moment upon her lips as she replied: 'Thou callest me  [4186]
to the greatest of all subjects of inquiry, a task for which the most  [4187]
exhaustive treatment barely suffices. Such is its nature that, as fast  [4188]
as one doubt is cut away, innumerable others spring up like Hydra's  [4189]
heads, nor could we set any limit to their renewal did we not apply the  [4190]
mind's living fire to suppress them. For there come within its scope the  [4191]
questions of the essential simplicity of providence, of the order of  [4192]
fate, of unforeseen chance, of the Divine knowledge and predestination,  [4193]
and of the freedom of the will. How heavy is the weight of all this  [4194]
thou canst judge for thyself. But, inasmuch as to know these things also  [4195]
is part of the treatment of thy malady, we will try to give them some  [4196]
consideration, despite the restrictions of the narrow limits of our  [4197]
time. Moreover, thou must for a time dispense with the pleasures of  [4198]
music and song, if so be that thou findest any delight therein, whilst I  [4199]
weave together the connected train of reasons in proper order.'  [4200]
  [4201]
'As thou wilt,' said I.  [4202]
  [4203]
Then, as if making a new beginning, she thus discoursed: 'The coming  [4204]
into being of all things, the whole course of development in things that  [4205]
change, every sort of thing that moves in any wise, receives its due  [4206]
cause, order, and form from the steadfastness of the Divine mind. This  [4207]
mind, calm in the citadel of its own essential simplicity, has decreed  [4208]
that the method of its rule shall be manifold. Viewed in the very purity  [4209]
of the Divine intelligence, this method is called _providence_; but  [4210]
viewed in regard to those things which it moves and disposes, it is  [4211]
what the ancients called _fate_. That these two are different will  [4212]
easily be clear to anyone who passes in review their respective  [4213]
efficacies. Providence is the Divine reason itself, seated in the  [4214]
Supreme Being, which disposes all things; fate is the disposition  [4215]
inherent in all things which move, through which providence joins all  [4216]
things in their proper order. Providence embraces all things, however  [4217]
different, however infinite; fate sets in motion separately individual  [4218]
things, and assigns to them severally their position, form, and time.  [4219]
  [4220]
'So the unfolding of this temporal order unified into the foreview of  [4221]
the Divine mind is providence, while the same unity broken up and  [4222]
unfolded in time is fate. And although these are different, yet is there  [4223]
a dependence between them; for the order of destiny issues from the  [4224]
essential simplicity of providence. For as the artificer, forming in his  [4225]
mind beforehand the idea of the thing to be made, carries out his  [4226]
design, and develops from moment to moment what he had before seen in a  [4227]
single instant as a whole, so God in His providence ordains all things  [4228]
as parts of a single unchanging whole, but carries out these very  [4229]
ordinances by fate in a time of manifold unity. So whether fate is  [4230]
accomplished by Divine spirits as the ministers of providence, or by a  [4231]
soul, or by the service of all nature--whether by the celestial motion  [4232]
of the stars, by the efficacy of angels, or by the many-sided cunning of  [4233]
demons--whether by all or by some of these the destined series is woven,  [4234]
this, at least, is manifest: that providence is the fixed and simple  [4235]
form of destined events, fate their shifting series in order of time, as  [4236]
by the disposal of the Divine simplicity they are to take place. Whereby  [4237]
it is that all things which are under fate are subjected also to  [4238]
providence, on which fate itself is dependent; whereas certain things  [4239]
which are set under providence are above the chain of fate--viz., those  [4240]
things which by their nearness to the primal Divinity are steadfastly  [4241]
fixed, and lie outside the order of fate's movements. For as the  [4242]
innermost of several circles revolving round the same centre approaches  [4243]
the simplicity of the midmost point, and is, as it were, a pivot round  [4244]
which the exterior circles turn, while the outermost, whirled in ampler  [4245]
orbit, takes in a wider and wider sweep of space in proportion to its  [4246]
departure from the indivisible unity of the centre--while, further,  [4247]
whatever joins and allies itself to the centre is narrowed to a like  [4248]
simplicity, and no longer expands vaguely into space--even so whatsoever  [4249]
departs widely from primal mind is involved more deeply in the meshes of  [4250]
fate, and things are free from fate in proportion as they seek to come  [4251]
nearer to that central pivot; while if aught cleaves close to supreme  [4252]
mind in its absolute fixity, this, too, being free from movement, rises  [4253]
above fate's necessity. Therefore, as is reasoning to pure intelligence,  [4254]
as that which is generated to that which is, time to eternity, a circle  [4255]
to its centre, so is the shifting series of fate to the steadfastness  [4256]
and simplicity of providence.  [4257]
  [4258]
'It is this causal series which moves heaven and the stars, attempers  [4259]
the elements to mutual accord, and again in turn transforms them into  [4260]
new combinations; _this_ which renews the series of all things that are  [4261]
born and die through like successions of germ and birth; it is _its_  [4262]
operation which binds the destinies of men by an indissoluble nexus of  [4263]
causality, and, since it issues in the beginning from unalterable  [4264]
providence, these destinies also must of necessity be immutable.  [4265]
Accordingly, the world is ruled for the best if this unity abiding in  [4266]
the Divine mind puts forth an inflexible order of causes. And this  [4267]
order, by its intrinsic immutability, restricts things mutable which  [4268]
otherwise would ebb and flow at random. And so it happens that, although  [4269]
to you, who are not altogether capable of understanding this order, all  [4270]
things seem confused and disordered, nevertheless there is everywhere an  [4271]
appointed limit which guides all things to good. Verily, nothing can be  [4272]
done for the sake of evil even by the wicked themselves; for, as we  [4273]
abundantly proved, they seek good, but are drawn out of the way by  [4274]
perverse error; far less can this order which sets out from the supreme  [4275]
centre of good turn aside anywhither from the way in which it began.  [4276]
  [4277]
'"Yet what confusion," thou wilt say, "can be more unrighteous than that  [4278]
prosperity and adversity should indifferently befall the good, what  [4279]
they like and what they loathe come alternately to the bad!" Yes; but  [4280]
have men in real life such soundness of mind that their judgments of  [4281]
righteousness and wickedness must necessarily correspond with facts?  [4282]
Why, on this very point their verdicts conflict, and those whom some  [4283]
deem worthy of reward, others deem worthy of punishment. Yet granted  [4284]
there were one who could rightly distinguish the good and bad, yet would  [4285]
he be able to look into the soul's inmost constitution, as it were, if  [4286]
we may borrow an expression used of the body? The marvel here is not  [4287]
unlike that which astonishes one who does not know why in health sweet  [4288]
things suit some constitutions, and bitter others, or why some sick men  [4289]
are best alleviated by mild remedies, others by severe. But the  [4290]
physician who distinguishes the precise conditions and characteristics  [4291]
of health and sickness does not marvel. Now, the health of the soul is  [4292]
nothing but righteousness, and vice is its sickness. God, the guide and  [4293]
physician of the mind, it is who preserves the good and banishes the  [4294]
bad. And He looks forth from the lofty watch-tower of His providence,  [4295]
perceives what is suited to each, and assigns what He knows to be  [4296]
suitable.  [4297]
  [4298]
'This, then, is what that extraordinary mystery of the order of destiny  [4299]
comes to--that something is done by one who knows, whereat the ignorant  [4300]
are astonished. But let us consider a few instances whereby appears what  [4301]
is the competency of human reason to fathom the Divine unsearchableness.  [4302]
Here is one whom thou deemest the perfection of justice and scrupulous  [4303]
integrity; to all-knowing Providence it seems far otherwise. We all know  [4304]
our Lucan's admonition that it was the winning cause that found favour  [4305]
with the gods, the beaten cause with Cato. So, shouldst thou see  [4306]
anything in this world happening differently from thy expectation, doubt  [4307]
not but events are rightly ordered; it is in thy judgment that there is  [4308]
perverse confusion.  [4309]
  [4310]
'Grant, however, there be somewhere found one of so happy a character  [4311]
that God and man alike agree in their judgments about him; yet is he  [4312]
somewhat infirm in strength of mind. It may be, if he fall into  [4313]
adversity, he will cease to practise that innocency which has failed to  [4314]
secure his fortune. Therefore, God's wise dispensation spares him whom  [4315]
adversity might make worse, will not let him suffer who is ill fitted  [4316]
for endurance. Another there is perfect in all virtue, so holy and nigh  [4317]
to God that providence judges it unlawful that aught untoward should  [4318]
befall him; nay, doth not even permit him to be afflicted with bodily  [4319]
disease. As one more excellent than I[N] hath said:  [4320]
  [4321]
'"The very body of the holy saint  [4322]
Is built of purest ether."  [4323]
  [4324]
Often it happens that the governance is given to the good that a  [4325]
restraint may be put upon superfluity of wickedness. To others  [4326]
providence assigns some mixed lot suited to their spiritual nature; some  [4327]
it will plague lest they grow rank through long prosperity; others it  [4328]
will suffer to be vexed with sore afflictions to confirm their virtues  [4329]
by the exercise and practice of patience. Some fear overmuch what they  [4330]
have strength to bear; others despise overmuch that to which their  [4331]
strength is unequal. All these it brings to the test of their true self  [4332]
through misfortune. Some also have bought a name revered to future ages  [4333]
at the price of a glorious death; some by invincible constancy under  [4334]
their sufferings have afforded an example to others that virtue cannot  [4335]
be overcome by calamity--all which things, without doubt, come to pass  [4336]
rightly and in due order, and to the benefit of those to whom they are  [4337]
seen to happen.  [4338]
  [4339]
'As to the other side of the marvel, that the bad now meet with  [4340]
affliction, now get their hearts' desire, this, too, springs from the  [4341]
same causes. As to the afflictions, of course no one marvels, because  [4342]
all hold the wicked to be ill deserving. The truth is, their punishments  [4343]
both frighten others from crime, and amend those on whom they are  [4344]
inflicted; while their prosperity is a powerful sermon to the good, what  [4345]
judgments they ought to pass on good fortune of this kind, which often  [4346]
attends the wicked so assiduously.  [4347]
  [4348]
'There is another object which may, I believe, be attained in such  [4349]
cases: there is one, perhaps, whose nature is so reckless and violent  [4350]
that poverty would drive him more desperately into crime. _His_ disorder  [4351]
providence relieves by allowing him to amass money. Such a one, in the  [4352]
uneasiness of a conscience stained with guilt, while he contrasts his  [4353]
character with his fortune, perchance grows alarmed lest he should come  [4354]
to mourn the loss of that whose possession is so pleasant to him. He  [4355]
will, then, reform his ways, and through the fear of losing his fortune  [4356]
he forsakes his iniquity. Some, through a prosperity unworthily borne,  [4357]
have been hurled headlong to ruin; to some the power of the sword has  [4358]
been committed, to the end that the good may be tried by discipline, and  [4359]
the bad punished. For while there can be no peace between the righteous  [4360]
and the wicked, neither can the wicked agree among themselves. How  [4361]
should they, when each is at variance with himself, because his vices  [4362]
rend his conscience, and ofttimes they do things which, when they are  [4363]
done, they judge ought not to have been done. Hence it is that this  [4364]
supreme providence brings to pass this notable marvel--that the bad make  [4365]
the bad good. For some, when they see the injustice which they  [4366]
themselves suffer at the hands of evil-doers, are inflamed with  [4367]
detestation of the offenders, and, in the endeavour to be unlike those  [4368]
whom they hate, return to the ways of virtue. It is the Divine power  [4369]
alone to which things evil are also good, in that, by putting them to  [4370]
suitable use, it bringeth them in the end to some good issue. For order  [4371]
in some way or other embraceth all things, so that even that which has  [4372]
departed from the appointed laws of the order, nevertheless falleth  [4373]
within _an_ order, though _another_ order, that nothing in the realm of  [4374]
providence may be left to haphazard. But  [4375]
  [4376]
'"Hard were the task, as a god, to recount all, nothing omitting."  [4377]
  [4378]
Nor, truly, is it lawful for man to compass in thought all the mechanism  [4379]
of the Divine work, or set it forth in speech. Let us be content to  [4380]
have apprehended this only--that God, the creator of universal nature,  [4381]
likewise disposeth all things, and guides them to good; and while He  [4382]
studies to preserve in likeness to Himself all that He has created, He  [4383]
banishes all evil from the borders of His commonweal through the links  [4384]
of fatal necessity. Whereby it comes to pass that, if thou look to  [4385]
disposing providence, thou wilt nowhere find the evils which are  [4386]
believed so to abound on earth.  [4387]
  [4388]
'But I see thou hast long been burdened with the weight of the subject,  [4389]
and fatigued with the prolixity of the argument, and now lookest for  [4390]
some refreshment of sweet poesy. Listen, then, and may the draught so  [4391]
restore thee that thou wilt bend thy mind more resolutely to what  [4392]
remains.'  [4393]

BOOK V.  [4622]
  [4623]
FREE WILL AND GOD'S FOREKNOWLEDGE.  [4624]
  [4625]
  [4626]
SUMMARY.  [4627]
  [4628]
CH. I. Boethius asks if there is really any such thing as chance.  [4629]
Philosophy answers, in conformity with Aristotle's definition  [4630]
(Phys., II. iv.), that chance is merely relative to human purpose,  [4631]
and that what seems fortuitous really depends on a more subtle form  [4632]
of causation.--CH. II. Has man, then, any freedom, if the reign of  [4633]
law is thus absolute? Freedom of choice, replies Philosophy, is a  [4634]
necessary attribute of reason. Man has a measure of freedom, though  [4635]
a less perfect freedom than divine natures.--CH. III. But how can  [4636]
man's freedom be reconciled with God's absolute foreknowledge? If  [4637]
God's foreknowledge be certain, it seems to exclude the possibility  [4638]
of man's free will. But if man has no freedom of choice, it  [4639]
follows that rewards and punishments are unjust as well as useless;  [4640]
that merit and demerit are mere names; that God is the cause of  [4641]
men's wickednesses; that prayer is meaningless.--CH. IV. The  [4642]
explanation is that man's reasoning faculties are not adequate to  [4643]
the apprehension of the ways of God's foreknowledge. If we could  [4644]
know, as He knows, all that is most perplexing in this problem  [4645]
would be made plain. For knowledge depends not on the nature of the  [4646]
thing known, but on the faculty of the knower.--CH. V. Now, where  [4647]
our senses conflict with our reason, we defer the judgment of the  [4648]
lower faculty to the judgment of the higher. Our present perplexity  [4649]
arises from our viewing God's foreknowledge from the standpoint of  [4650]
human reason. We must try and rise to the higher standpoint of  [4651]
God's immediate intuition.--CH. VI. To understand this higher form  [4652]
of cognition, we must consider God's nature. God is eternal.  [4653]
Eternity is more than mere everlasting duration. Accordingly, His  [4654]
knowledge surveys past and future in the timelessness of an eternal  [4655]
present. His foreseeing is seeing. Yet this foreseeing does not in  [4656]
itself impose necessity, any more than our seeing things happen  [4657]
makes their happening necessary. We may, however, if we please,  [4658]
distinguish two necessities--one absolute, the other conditional on  [4659]
knowledge. In this conditional sense alone do the things which God  [4660]
foresees necessarily come to pass. But this kind of necessity  [4661]
affects not the nature of things. It leaves the reality of free  [4662]
will unimpaired, and the evils feared do not ensue. Our  [4663]
responsibility is great, since all that we do is done in the sight  [4664]
of all-seeing Providence.  [4665]

She ceased, and was about to pass on in her discourse to the exposition  [4677]

but now, beset the question of providence. I want to know whether thou  [4681]
deemest that there is any such thing as chance at all, and, if so, what  [4682]
it is.'  [4683]
  [4684]
Then she made answer: 'I am anxious to fulfil my promise completely, and  [4685]
open to thee a way of return to thy native land. As for these matters,  [4686]
though very useful to know, they are yet a little removed from the path  [4687]
of our design, and I fear lest digressions should fatigue thee, and thou  [4688]
shouldst find thyself unequal to completing the direct journey to our  [4689]
goal.'  [4690]
  [4691]
'Have no fear for that,' said I. 'It is rest to me to learn, where  [4692]
learning brings delight so exquisite, especially when thy argument has  [4693]
been built up on all sides with undoubted conviction, and no place is  [4694]
left for uncertainty in what follows.'  [4695]
  [4696]
She made answer: 'I will accede to thy request;' and forthwith she thus  [4697]
began: 'If chance be defined as a result produced by random movement  [4698]
without any link of causal connection, I roundly affirm that there is no  [4699]
such thing as chance at all, and consider the word to be altogether  [4700]
without meaning, except as a symbol of the thing designated. What place  [4701]
can be left for random action, when God constraineth all things to  [4702]
order? For "ex nihilo nihil" is sound doctrine which none of the  [4703]
ancients gainsaid, although they used it of material substance, not of  [4704]
the efficient principle; this they laid down as a kind of basis for all  [4705]
their reasonings concerning nature. Now, if a thing arise without  [4706]
causes, it will appear to have arisen from nothing. But if this cannot  [4707]
be, neither is it possible for there to be chance in accordance with the  [4708]
definition just given.'  [4709]
  [4710]
'Well,' said I, 'is there, then, nothing which can properly be called  [4711]
chance or accident, or is there something to which these names are  [4712]
appropriate, though its nature is dark to the vulgar?'  [4713]
  [4714]
'Our good Aristotle,' says she, 'has defined it concisely in his  [4715]
"Physics," and closely in accordance with the truth.'  [4716]
  [4717]
'How, pray?' said I.  [4718]
  [4719]
'Thus,' says she: 'Whenever something is done for the sake of a  [4720]
particular end, and for certain reasons some other result than that  [4721]
designed ensues, this is called chance; for instance, if a man is  [4722]
digging the earth for tillage, and finds a mass of buried gold. Now,  [4723]
such a find is regarded as accidental; yet it is not "ex nihilo," for it  [4724]
has its proper causes, the unforeseen and unexpected concurrence of  [4725]
which has brought the chance about. For had not the cultivator been  [4726]
digging, had not the man who hid the money buried it in that precise  [4727]
spot, the gold would not have been found. These, then, are the reasons  [4728]
why the find is a chance one, in that it results from causes which met  [4729]
together and concurred, not from any intention on the part of the  [4730]
discoverer. Since neither he who buried the gold nor he who worked in  [4731]
the field _intended_ that the money should be found, but, as I said, it  [4732]
_happened_ by coincidence that one dug where the other buried the  [4733]
treasure. We may, then, define chance as being an unexpected result  [4734]
flowing from a concurrence of causes where the several factors had some  [4735]
definite end. But the meeting and concurrence of these causes arises  [4736]
from that inevitable chain of order which, flowing from the  [4737]
fountain-head of Providence, disposes all things in their due time and  [4738]
place.'  [4739]

III.  [4843]
  [4844]
  [4845]
Then said I: 'But now I am once more perplexed by a problem yet more  [4846]
difficult.'  [4847]
  [4848]
'And what is that?' said she; 'yet, in truth, I can guess what it is  [4849]
that troubles you.'  [4850]
  [4851]
'It seems,' said I, 'too much of a paradox and a contradiction that God  [4852]
should know all things, and yet there should be free will. For if God  [4853]
foresees everything, and can in no wise be deceived, that which  [4854]
providence foresees to be about to happen must necessarily come to pass.  [4855]
Wherefore, if from eternity He foreknows not only what men will do, but  [4856]
also their designs and purposes, there can be no freedom of the will,  [4857]
seeing that nothing can be done, nor can any sort of purpose be  [4858]
entertained, save such as a Divine providence, incapable of being  [4859]
deceived, has perceived beforehand. For if the issues can be turned  [4860]
aside to some other end than that foreseen by providence, there will not  [4861]
then be any sure foreknowledge of the future, but uncertain conjecture  [4862]
instead, and to think this of God I deem impiety.  [4863]
  [4864]
'Moreover, I do not approve the reasoning by which some think to solve  [4865]
this puzzle. For they say that it is not because God has foreseen the  [4866]
coming of an event that _therefore_ it is sure to come to pass, but,  [4867]
conversely, because something is about to come to pass, it cannot be  [4868]
hidden from Divine providence; and accordingly the necessity passes to  [4869]
the opposite side, and it is not that what is foreseen must necessarily  [4870]
come to pass, but that what is about to come to pass must necessarily be  [4871]
foreseen. But this is just as if the matter in debate were, which is  [4872]
cause and which effect--whether foreknowledge of the future cause of the  [4873]
necessity, or the necessity of the future of the foreknowledge. But we  [4874]
need not be at the pains of demonstrating that, whatsoever be the order  [4875]
of the causal sequence, the occurrence of things foreseen is necessary,  [4876]
even though the foreknowledge of future events does not in itself  [4877]
impose upon them the necessity of their occurrence. For example, if a  [4878]
man be seated, the supposition of his being seated is necessarily true;  [4879]
and, conversely, if the supposition of his being seated is true, because  [4880]
he is really seated, he must necessarily be sitting. So, in either case,  [4881]
there is some necessity involved--in this latter case, the necessity of  [4882]
the fact; in the former, of the truth of the statement. But in both  [4883]
cases the sitter is not therefore seated because the opinion is true,  [4884]
but rather the opinion is true because antecedently he was sitting as a  [4885]
matter of fact. Thus, though the cause of the truth of the opinion comes  [4886]
from the other side,[P] yet there is a necessity on both sides alike. We  [4887]
can obviously reason similarly in the case of providence and the future.  [4888]
Even if future events are foreseen because they are about to happen, and  [4889]
do not come to pass because they are foreseen, still, all the same,  [4890]
there is a necessity, both that they should be foreseen by God as about  [4891]
to come to pass, and that when they are foreseen they should happen, and  [4892]
this is sufficient for the destruction of free will. However, it is  [4893]
preposterous to speak of the occurrence of events in time as the cause  [4894]
of eternal foreknowledge. And yet if we believe that God foresees future  [4895]
events because they are about to come to pass, what is it but to think  [4896]
that the occurrence of events is the cause of His supreme providence?  [4897]
Further, just as when I _know_ that anything is, that thing  [4898]
_necessarily_ is, so when I know that anything will be, it will  [4899]
_necessarily_ be. It follows, then, that things foreknown come to pass  [4900]
inevitably.  [4901]
  [4902]
'Lastly, to think of a thing as being in any way other than what it is,  [4903]
is not only not knowledge, but it is false opinion widely different from  [4904]
the truth of knowledge. Consequently, if anything is about to be, and  [4905]
yet its occurrence is not certain and necessary, how can anyone foreknow  [4906]
that it will occur? For just as knowledge itself is free from all  [4907]
admixture of falsity, so any conception drawn from knowledge cannot be  [4908]
other than as it is conceived. For this, indeed, is the cause why  [4909]
knowledge is free from falsehood, because of necessity each thing must  [4910]
correspond exactly with the knowledge which grasps its nature. In what  [4911]
way, then, are we to suppose that God foreknows these uncertainties as  [4912]
about to come to pass? For if He thinks of events which possibly may not  [4913]
happen at all as inevitably destined to come to pass, He is deceived;  [4914]
and this it is not only impious to believe, but even so much as to  [4915]
express in words. If, on the other hand, He sees them in the future as  [4916]
they are in such a sense as to know that they may equally come to pass  [4917]
or not, what sort of foreknowledge is this which comprehends nothing  [4918]
certain nor fixed? What better is this than the absurd vaticination of  [4919]
Teiresias?  [4920]
  [4921]
'"Whate'er I say  [4922]
Shall either come to pass--or not."  [4923]
  [4924]
In that case, too, in what would Divine providence surpass human opinion  [4925]
if it holds for uncertain things the occurrence of which is uncertain,  [4926]
even as men do? But if at that perfectly sure Fountain-head of all  [4927]
things no shadow of uncertainty can possibly be found, then the  [4928]
occurrence of those things which He has surely foreknown as coming is  [4929]
certain. Wherefore there can be no freedom in human actions and designs;  [4930]
but the Divine mind, which foresees all things without possibility of  [4931]
mistake, ties and binds them down to one only issue. But this admission  [4932]
once made, what an upset of human affairs manifestly ensues! Vainly are  [4933]
rewards and punishments proposed for the good and bad, since no free and  [4934]
voluntary motion of the will has deserved either one or the other; nay,  [4935]
the punishment of the wicked and the reward of the righteous, which is  [4936]
now esteemed the perfection of justice, will seem the most flagrant  [4937]
injustice, since men are determined either way not by their own proper  [4938]
volition, but by the necessity of what must surely be. And therefore  [4939]
neither virtue nor vice is anything, but rather good and ill desert are  [4940]
confounded together without distinction. Moreover, seeing that the whole  [4941]
course of events is deduced from providence, and nothing is left free to  [4942]
human design, it comes to pass that our vices also are referred to the  [4943]
Author of all good--a thought than which none more abominable can  [4944]
possibly be conceived. Again, no ground is left for hope or prayer,  [4945]
since how can we hope for blessings, or pray for mercy, when every  [4946]
object of desire depends upon the links of an unalterable chain of  [4947]
causation? Gone, then, is the one means of intercourse between God and  [4948]
man--the communion of hope and prayer--if it be true that we ever earn  [4949]
the inestimable recompense of the Divine favour at the price of a due  [4950]
humility; for this is the one way whereby men seem able to hold  [4951]
communion with God, and are joined to that unapproachable light by the  [4952]
very act of supplication, even before they obtain their petitions. Then,  [4953]
since these things can scarcely be believed to have any efficacy, if the  [4954]
necessity of future events be admitted, what means will there be whereby  [4955]
we may be brought near and cleave to Him who is the supreme Head of all?  [4956]
Wherefore it needs must be that the human race, even as thou didst  [4957]
erstwhile declare in song, parted and dissevered from its Source, should  [4958]
fall to ruin.'  [4959]

vigorously discussed by Cicero in his "Divination"; thou also hast long  [5025]
and earnestly pondered the problem, yet no one has had diligence and  [5026]

is that the movement of human reasoning cannot cope with the simplicity  [5028]
of the Divine foreknowledge; for if a conception of its nature could in  [5029]
any wise be framed, no shadow of uncertainty would remain. With a view  [5030]
of making this at last clear and plain, I will begin by considering the  [5031]
arguments by which thou art swayed. First, I inquire into the reasons  [5032]
why thou art dissatisfied with the solution proposed, which is to the  [5033]
effect that, seeing the fact of foreknowledge is not thought the cause  [5034]
of the necessity of future events, foreknowledge is not to be deemed any  [5035]
hindrance to the freedom of the will. Now, surely the sole ground on  [5036]
which thou arguest the necessity of the future is that things which are  [5037]
foreknown cannot fail to come to pass. But if, as thou wert ready to  [5038]
acknowledge just now, the fact of foreknowledge imposes no necessity on  [5039]
things future, what reason is there for supposing the results of  [5040]
voluntary action constrained to a fixed issue? Suppose, for the sake of  [5041]
argument, and to see what follows, we assume that there is no  [5042]
foreknowledge. Are willed actions, then, tied down to any necessity in  [5043]
_this_ case?'  [5044]
  [5045]
'Certainly not.'  [5046]
  [5047]
'Let us assume foreknowledge again, but without its involving any actual  [5048]
necessity; the freedom of the will, I imagine, will remain in complete  [5049]
integrity. But thou wilt say that, even although the foreknowledge is  [5050]
not the necessity of the future event's occurrence, yet it is a sign  [5051]
that it will necessarily happen. Granted; but in this case it is plain  [5052]
that, even if there had been no foreknowledge, the issues would have  [5053]
been inevitably certain. For a sign only indicates something which is,  [5054]
does not bring to pass that of which it is the sign. We require to show  [5055]
beforehand that all things, without exception, happen of necessity in  [5056]
order that a preconception may be a sign of this necessity. Otherwise,  [5057]
if there is no such universal necessity, neither can any preconception  [5058]
be a sign of a necessity which exists not. Manifestly, too, a proof  [5059]
established on firm grounds of reason must be drawn not from signs and  [5060]
loose general arguments, but from suitable and necessary causes. But how  [5061]
can it be that things foreseen should ever fail to come to pass? Why,  [5062]
this is to suppose us to believe that the events which providence  [5063]
foresees to be coming were not about to happen, instead of our supposing  [5064]
that, although they should come to pass, yet there was no necessity  [5065]
involved in their own nature compelling their occurrence. Take an  [5066]
illustration that will help to convey my meaning. There are many things  [5067]
which we see taking place before our eyes--the movements of charioteers,  [5068]
for instance, in guiding and turning their cars, and so on. Now, is any  [5069]
one of these movements compelled by any necessity?'  [5070]
  [5071]
'No; certainly not. There would be no efficacy in skill if all motions  [5072]
took place perforce.'  [5073]
  [5074]
'Then, things which in taking place are free from any necessity as to  [5075]
their being in the present must also, before they take place, be about  [5076]
to happen without necessity. Wherefore there are things which will come  [5077]
to pass, the occurrence of which is perfectly free from necessity. At  [5078]
all events, I imagine that no one will deny that things now taking place  [5079]
were about to come to pass before they were actually happening. Such  [5080]
things, however much foreknown, are in their occurrence _free_. For even  [5081]
as knowledge of things present imports no necessity into things that are  [5082]
taking place, so foreknowledge of the future imports none into things  [5083]
that are about to come. But this, thou wilt say, is the very point in  [5084]
dispute--whether any foreknowing is possible of things whose occurrence  [5085]
is not necessary. For here there seems to thee a contradiction, and, if  [5086]
they are foreseen, their necessity follows; whereas if there is no  [5087]
necessity, they can by no means be foreknown; and thou thinkest that  [5088]
nothing can be grasped as known unless it is certain, but if things  [5089]
whose occurrence is uncertain are foreknown as certain, this is the very  [5090]
mist of opinion, not the truth of knowledge. For to think of things  [5091]
otherwise than as they are, thou believest to be incompatible with the  [5092]
soundness of knowledge.  [5093]
  [5094]
'Now, the cause of the mistake is this--that men think that all  [5095]
knowledge is cognized purely by the nature and efficacy of the thing  [5096]
known. Whereas the case is the very reverse: all that is known is  [5097]
grasped not conformably to its own efficacy, but rather conformably to  [5098]
the faculty of the knower. An example will make this clear: the  [5099]
roundness of a body is recognised in one way by sight, in another by  [5100]
touch. Sight looks upon it from a distance as a whole by a simultaneous  [5101]
reflection of rays; touch grasps the roundness piecemeal, by contact and  [5102]
attachment to the surface, and by actual movement round the periphery  [5103]
itself. Man himself, likewise, is viewed in one way by Sense, in another  [5104]
by Imagination, in another way, again, by Thought, in another by pure  [5105]
Intelligence. Sense judges figure clothed in material substance,  [5106]
Imagination figure alone without matter. Thought transcends this again,  [5107]
and by its contemplation of universals considers the type itself which  [5108]
is contained in the individual. The eye of Intelligence is yet more  [5109]
exalted; for overpassing the sphere of the universal, it will behold  [5110]
absolute form itself by the pure force of the mind's vision. Wherein the  [5111]
main point to be considered is this: the higher faculty of comprehension  [5112]
embraces the lower, while the lower cannot rise to the higher. For Sense  [5113]
has no efficacy beyond matter, nor can Imagination behold universal  [5114]
ideas, nor Thought embrace pure form; but Intelligence, looking down, as  [5115]
it were, from its higher standpoint in its intuition of form,  [5116]
discriminates also the several elements which underlie it; but it  [5117]
comprehends them in the same way as it comprehends that form itself,  [5118]
which could be cognized by no other than itself. For it cognizes the  [5119]
universal of Thought, the figure of Imagination, and the matter of  [5120]
Sense, without employing Thought, Imagination, or Sense, but surveying  [5121]
all things, so to speak, under the aspect of pure form by a single flash  [5122]
of intuition. Thought also, in considering the universal, embraces  [5123]
images and sense-impressions without resorting to Imagination or Sense.  [5124]
For it is Thought which has thus defined the universal from its  [5125]
conceptual point of view: "Man is a two-legged animal endowed with  [5126]
reason." This is indeed a universal notion, yet no one is ignorant that  [5127]
the _thing_ is imaginable and presentable to Sense, because Thought  [5128]
considers it not by Imagination or Sense, but by means of rational  [5129]
conception. Imagination, too, though its faculty of viewing and forming  [5130]
representations is founded upon the senses, nevertheless surveys  [5131]
sense-impressions without calling in Sense, not in the way of  [5132]
Sense-perception, but of Imagination. See'st thou, then, how all things  [5133]
in cognizing use rather their own faculty than the faculty of the things  [5134]
which they cognize? Nor is this strange; for since every judgment is the  [5135]
act of the judge, it is necessary that each should accomplish its task  [5136]
by its own, not by another's power.'  [5137]

'Now, although in the case of bodies endowed with sentiency the  [5209]

belong to distinct and different substances. For to creatures void of  [5219]

pertains only to the human race, as Intelligence to Divinity alone;  [5224]
hence it follows that that form of knowledge exceeds the rest which of  [5225]
its own nature cognizes not only its proper object, but the objects of  [5226]
the other forms of knowledge also. But what if Sense and Imagination  [5227]
were to gainsay Thought, and declare that universal which Thought deems  [5228]
itself to behold to be nothing? For the object of Sense and Imagination  [5229]
cannot be universal; so that either the judgment of Reason is true and  [5230]

objects are presented to Sense and Imagination, the conception of  [5232]
Reason, which looks on that which is perceived by Sense and particular  [5233]
as if it were a something "universal," is empty of content. Suppose,  [5234]
further, that Reason maintains in reply that it does indeed contemplate  [5235]
the object of both Sense and Imagination under the form of  [5236]
universality, while Sense and Imagination cannot aspire to the  [5237]
knowledge of the universal, since their cognizance cannot go beyond  [5238]
bodily figures, and that in the cognition of reality we ought rather to  [5239]
trust the stronger and more perfect faculty of judgment. In a dispute of  [5240]
this sort, should not we, in whom is planted the faculty of reasoning as  [5241]
well as of imagining and perceiving, espouse the cause of Reason?  [5242]

  [5289]
  [5290]
'Since, then, as we lately proved, everything that is known is cognized  [5291]
not in accordance with its own nature, but in accordance with the nature  [5292]
of the faculty that comprehends it, let us now contemplate, as far as  [5293]
lawful, the character of the Divine essence, that we may be able to  [5294]
understand also the nature of its knowledge.  [5295]
  [5296]
'God is eternal; in this judgment all rational beings agree. Let us,  [5297]
then, consider what eternity is. For this word carries with it a  [5298]
revelation alike of the Divine nature and of the Divine knowledge. Now,  [5299]
eternity is the possession of endless life whole and perfect at a single  [5300]
moment. What this is becomes more clear and manifest from a comparison  [5301]
with things temporal. For whatever lives in time is a present proceeding  [5302]
from the past to the future, and there is nothing set in time which can  [5303]
embrace the whole space of its life together. To-morrow's state it  [5304]
grasps not yet, while it has already lost yesterday's; nay, even in the  [5305]
life of to-day ye live no longer than one brief transitory moment.  [5306]
Whatever, therefore, is subject to the condition of time, although, as  [5307]
Aristotle deemed of the world, it never have either beginning or end,  [5308]
and its life be stretched to the whole extent of time's infinity, it yet  [5309]
is not such as rightly to be thought eternal. For it does not include  [5310]
and embrace the whole space of infinite life at once, but has no present  [5311]
hold on things to come, not yet accomplished. Accordingly, that which  [5312]
includes and possesses the whole fulness of unending life at once, from  [5313]
which nothing future is absent, from which nothing past has escaped,  [5314]
this is rightly called eternal; this must of necessity be ever present  [5315]
to itself in full self-possession, and hold the infinity of movable time  [5316]
in an abiding present. Wherefore they deem not rightly who imagine that  [5317]
on Plato's principles the created world is made co-eternal with the  [5318]
Creator, because they are told that he believed the world to have had  [5319]
no beginning in time,[S] and to be destined never to come to an end. For  [5320]
it is one thing for existence to be endlessly prolonged, which was what  [5321]
Plato ascribed to the world, another for the whole of an endless life to  [5322]
be embraced in the present, which is manifestly a property peculiar to  [5323]
the Divine mind. Nor need God appear earlier in mere duration of time to  [5324]
created things, but only prior in the unique simplicity of His nature.  [5325]
For the infinite progression of things in time copies this immediate  [5326]
existence in the present of the changeless life, and when it cannot  [5327]
succeed in equalling it, declines from movelessness into motion, and  [5328]
falls away from the simplicity of a perpetual present to the infinite  [5329]
duration of the future and the past; and since it cannot possess the  [5330]
whole fulness of its life together, for the very reason that in a manner  [5331]
it never ceases to be, it seems, up to a certain point, to rival that  [5332]
which it cannot complete and express by attaching itself indifferently  [5333]
to any present moment of time, however swift and brief; and since this  [5334]
bears some resemblance to that ever-abiding present, it bestows on  [5335]
everything to which it is assigned the semblance of existence. But since  [5336]
it cannot abide, it hurries along the infinite path of time, and the  [5337]
result has been that it continues by ceaseless movement the life the  [5338]
completeness of which it could not embrace while it stood still. So, if  [5339]
we are minded to give things their right names, we shall follow Plato in  [5340]
saying that God indeed is eternal, but the world everlasting.  [5341]
  [5342]
'Since, then, every mode of judgment comprehends its objects conformably  [5343]
to its own nature, and since God abides for ever in an eternal present,  [5344]
His knowledge, also transcending all movement of time, dwells in the  [5345]
simplicity of its own changeless present, and, embracing the whole  [5346]
infinite sweep of the past and of the future, contemplates all that  [5347]
falls within its simple cognition as if it were now taking place. And  [5348]
therefore, if thou wilt carefully consider that immediate presentment  [5349]
whereby it discriminates all things, thou wilt more rightly deem it not  [5350]
foreknowledge as of something future, but knowledge of a moment that  [5351]
never passes. For this cause the name chosen to describe it is not  [5352]
prevision, but providence, because, since utterly removed in nature from  [5353]
things mean and trivial, its outlook embraces all things as from some  [5354]
lofty height. Why, then, dost thou insist that the things which are  [5355]
surveyed by the Divine eye are involved in necessity, whereas clearly  [5356]
men impose no necessity on things which they see? Does the act of vision  [5357]
add any necessity to the things which thou seest before thy eyes?'  [5358]
  [5359]
'Assuredly not.'  [5360]
  [5361]
'And yet, if we may without unfitness compare God's present and man's,  [5362]
just as ye see certain things in this your temporary present, so does He  [5363]
see all things in His eternal present. Wherefore this Divine  [5364]
anticipation changes not the natures and properties of things, and it  [5365]
beholds things present before it, just as they will hereafter come to  [5366]
pass in time. Nor does it confound things in its judgment, but in the  [5367]
one mental view distinguishes alike what will come necessarily and what  [5368]
without necessity. For even as ye, when at one and the same time ye see  [5369]
a man walking on the earth and the sun rising in the sky, distinguish  [5370]
between the two, though one glance embraces both, and judge the former  [5371]
voluntary, the latter necessary action: so also the Divine vision in its  [5372]
universal range of view does in no wise confuse the characters of the  [5373]
things which are present to its regard, though future in respect of  [5374]
time. Whence it follows that when it perceives that something will come  [5375]
into existence, and yet is perfectly aware that this is unbound by any  [5376]
necessity, its apprehension is not opinion, but rather knowledge based  [5377]
on truth. And if to this thou sayest that what God sees to be about to  [5378]
come to pass cannot fail to come to pass, and that what cannot fail to  [5379]
come to pass happens of necessity, and wilt tie me down to this word  [5380]
necessity, I will acknowledge that thou affirmest a most solid truth,  [5381]
but one which scarcely anyone can approach to who has not made the  [5382]
Divine his special study. For my answer would be that the same future  [5383]
event is necessary from the standpoint of Divine knowledge, but when  [5384]
considered in its own nature it seems absolutely free and unfettered.  [5385]
So, then, there are two necessities--one simple, as that men are  [5386]
necessarily mortal; the other conditioned, as that, if you know that  [5387]
someone is walking, he must necessarily be walking. For that which is  [5388]
known cannot indeed be otherwise than as it is known to be, and yet this  [5389]
fact by no means carries with it that other simple necessity. For the  [5390]
former necessity is not imposed by the thing's own proper nature, but by  [5391]
the addition of a condition. No necessity compels one who is voluntarily  [5392]
walking to go forward, although it is necessary for him to go forward at  [5393]
the moment of walking. In the same way, then, if Providence sees  [5394]
anything as present, that must necessarily be, though it is bound by no  [5395]
necessity of nature. Now, God views as present those coming events which  [5396]
happen of free will. These, accordingly, from the standpoint of the  [5397]
Divine vision are made necessary conditionally on the Divine  [5398]
cognizance; viewed, however, in themselves, they desist not from the  [5399]
absolute freedom naturally theirs. Accordingly, without doubt, all  [5400]
things will come to pass which God foreknows as about to happen, but of  [5401]
these certain proceed of free will; and though these happen, yet by the  [5402]
fact of their existence they do not lose their proper nature, in virtue  [5403]
of which before they happened it was really possible that they might not  [5404]
have come to pass.  [5405]
  [5406]
'What difference, then, does the denial of necessity make, since,  [5407]
through their being conditioned by Divine knowledge, they come to pass  [5408]
as if they were in all respects under the compulsion of necessity? This  [5409]
difference, surely, which we saw in the case of the instances I formerly  [5410]
took, the sun's rising and the man's walking; which at the moment of  [5411]
their occurrence could not but be taking place, and yet one of them  [5412]
before it took place was necessarily obliged to be, while the other was  [5413]
not so at all. So likewise the things which to God are present without  [5414]
doubt exist, but some of them come from the necessity of things, others  [5415]
from the power of the agent. Quite rightly, then, have we said that  [5416]
these things are necessary if viewed from the standpoint of the Divine  [5417]
knowledge; but if they are considered in themselves, they are free from  [5418]
the bonds of necessity, even as everything which is accessible to sense,  [5419]
regarded from the standpoint of Thought, is universal, but viewed in its  [5420]
own nature particular. "But," thou wilt say, "if it is in my power to  [5421]
change my purpose, I shall make void providence, since I shall perchance  [5422]
change something which comes within its foreknowledge." My answer is:  [5423]
Thou canst indeed turn aside thy purpose; but since the truth of  [5424]
providence is ever at hand to see that thou canst, and whether thou  [5425]
dost, and whither thou turnest thyself, thou canst not avoid the Divine  [5426]
foreknowledge, even as thou canst not escape the sight of a present  [5427]
spectator, although of thy free will thou turn thyself to various  [5428]
actions. Wilt thou, then, say: "Shall the Divine knowledge be changed at  [5429]
my discretion, so that, when I will this or that, providence changes its  [5430]
knowledge correspondingly?"  [5431]
  [5432]
'Surely not.'  [5433]
  [5434]
'True, for the Divine vision anticipates all that is coming, and  [5435]
transforms and reduces it to the form of its own present knowledge, and  [5436]
varies not, as thou deemest, in its foreknowledge, alternating to this  [5437]
or that, but in a single flash it forestalls and includes thy mutations  [5438]
without altering. And this ever-present comprehension and survey of all  [5439]
things God has received, not from the issue of future events, but from  [5440]
the simplicity of His own nature. Hereby also is resolved the objection  [5441]
which a little while ago gave thee offence--that our doings in the  [5442]
future were spoken of as if supplying the cause of God's knowledge. For  [5443]
this faculty of knowledge, embracing all things in its immediate  [5444]
cognizance, has itself fixed the bounds of all things, yet itself owes  [5445]
nothing to what comes after.  [5446]
  [5447]
'And all this being so, the freedom of man's will stands unshaken, and  [5448]
laws are not unrighteous, since their rewards and punishments are held  [5449]
forth to wills unbound by any necessity. God, who foreknoweth all  [5450]
things, still looks down from above, and the ever-present eternity of  [5451]
His vision concurs with the future character of all our acts, and  [5452]
dispenseth to the good rewards, to the bad punishments. Our hopes and  [5453]
prayers also are not fixed on God in vain, and when they are rightly  [5454]
directed cannot fail of effect. Therefore, withstand vice, practise  [5455]
virtue, lift up your souls to right hopes, offer humble prayers to  [5456]
Heaven. Great is the necessity of righteousness laid upon you if ye will  [5457]
not hide it from yourselves, seeing that all your actions are done  [5458]
before the eyes of a Judge who seeth all things.'  [5459]
